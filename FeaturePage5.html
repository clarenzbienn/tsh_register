Rising Price of Rice: Impact on Filipino’s Dining TableBy Mark Daniel L. Barcelona
Photo from https://www.bworldonline.com/economy/2023/03/15/510902/rising-rice-prices-reflect-increased-fertilizer-costs-farmers-group-says/#google_vignette
In a bustling market, on a young Saturday evening, Juan dela Cruz fought his way towards the swarm of people in front of a sari-sari store. Dust covered his feet and slippers, as he had just finished working in a construction site. Counting the remaining change from buying the items on his good wife’s list, his face lifted when only one was left: a kilogram of rice. As he made his way through, he looked at the variety of rice and scanned for the lowest-priced one that could fit in what remains of his daily wage clinking in his pocket. Juan dela Cruz’s face grew weary and the continuous rise in the price of these white grains, for times he could not count, bore extra weight on his shoulder. He could not do anything, so he bought half a kilogram and headed home.Juan dela Cruz’s story seems to be literally every Filipino’s story now.

Rice is a staple food in a Filipino family’s dining table. Almost every meal, rice is always served and paired with different dishes. According to the National Nutrition Council (2020), rice has both cultural and nutritional importance to Filipinos. It is associated with traditions and practices rooted deep into the national identity. It also provides half the calorie requirements and other essential nutrients needed daily. As an agricultural country whose diet relies upon this crop, an increase, even just a couple of pesos, can greatly affect the budget – especially of the average and those in and below the poverty line.

In a report of Cariaso (2023), for One News, there has only been increase in the price of rice since April of this year. The lowest that could be bought in the market is at forty pesos (Php 40.00) and in Metro Manila, it can spike up to forty-six pesos (Php 46.00). The causes are intertwining but reports revealed that rice is sold at an extremely low farmgate price. There are laws implemented by the government aiming to improve food security, such as the Republic Act No. 11203 or the Rice

Liberalization Law, but it has failed to tame the increasing price. IBON Foundation (2023) expressed their sentiments regarding the law as only putting threat to the local farmers and aggravating dependence of the country in importing rice.

The rise in the price of Filipino’s staple food is more than just losing capability of buying enough to last for a day. It is a constant source of worry and anxiousness of not having something to feed the family. It means doing extended hours of work and taking on extra jobs to cope up with the ever-increasing price. Yet, most certainly, every Juan dela Cruz, still holds on tight to the hope of a quality and affordable rice for the country.

After all, “Mawala na ang lahat, ‘wag lang ang kanin.’
