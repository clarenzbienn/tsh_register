Off to the races By Roxan D. Resuello
Photo from https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferrari_F1-75
Packed with incredible and mind-blowing high-speed overtakes, a variety of challenging race-track layouts, a 90-minute hot showcase of fast reflexes topped with high-octane driving skills, and a top-of-the-notch technology fueling the hybrid cars, it is without a doubt that Formula 1 is at the top-shelf of motorsports and is therefore worthy of global appreciation and attention.With its unparalleled technological advancements and intricate teamwork between the driver, the pit wall's engineers, and strategists, and the garage's mechanics, the race has become a global spectacle for spectators looking for an adrenaline-filled sport to entertain themselves.
A hundred-year-old historyDespite only reaching its peak popularity in the mainstream media in recent years, Formula One automobile racing's history can be traced back as far as 100 years ago in pre-World War France. Since then, the sport has evolved from its race cars with "rudimentary machines, simple chassis, and engines that were little more than modified road cars" to racing cars powered by hybrid engines and adapted with the most recent aerodynamic advancements and sustainable technology.

With its old roots being only formerly associated with the upper-class motor enthusiasts and wealthy manufacturers of the 1900s, the sport has slowly made its presence more inclusive and accessible to everyone regardless of class and status.

Watching open-wheel racing has become a prominent talking point and weekend activity for the newer generations, especially among the millennials and members of Generation Z. A familiar sportAlthough the sport is a relatively familiar idea, Formula 1 has never been a household name for most of the world, even in the Philippines. The common Filipinos somehow know single-seat auto racing as an established theme in birthday parties, and to some extent, it is existent with PSP games, video games, and movies, which shows a little more than less excitement than what exactly the race has to offer. The Grand Prix Formula 1 is the world's fastest and most advanced motorsport, often called the "pinnacle of motorsports," where cars race at an asphalt track with speeds averaging 220 miles per hour. The sports run on a strict and complex implementation of rules and guidelines on-track racing, testing, and developments governed and sanctioned by a world body called the FIA - Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile.

Basically, it is a battle of quick wits and teamwork. Ten teams with two drivers make up the "grid" and compete with each other in an open-cockpit professional motor racing contest. An engineer for each driver is assigned through the radio to guide and communicate team orders during the race. The main goal of each team is to compete with their technologically advanced cars and gain as many points as possible to win the Constructor's Championship. The 20 race drivers, however, subject themselves to a wheel-to-wheel battle to win the World Driver's Championship based on an individual performance point-grade system.An action-packed weekendAn F1 season comprises several race events, called the Grand Prix, held on a 3-day stretch at international circuits around the world, most of which are found in major cities in Europe and the Americas.

The 3-day event is divided into three free practice sessions held on Friday and Saturday. FP1, FP2, and FP3 last an hour each for the team to gather relevant data and optimize the car for the Qualifying Sessions, which are scheduled later on Saturdays. Qualifying sessions determine the driver's starting grid position for Sunday's race by competing to set the fastest complete lap times against the clock. The result during QS is detrimental to securing an opportunity to win the actual race on Sunday.

Sprint races are also part of the event. However, it is only done at specific circuits and GPs.
More than a high-speed eventThe misconception that motor racing is nothing but watching cars go full-throttle multiple times in a circuit undermines the efforts of the people working behind the science of the car's automation and the impeccable analysis and proactive strategic management of strategists and engineers. True that the star of the event is the cars and their pilots, but behind the speed and smoke comes a hotpot of complex data and intricate decision-making on devising an optimal plan, may it be deciding which type of tires to use or which race strategy should be played out, all while securing the team's triumph and the driver's safety at hand.
Pushing toward a more inclusive environmentDespite several issues relating to misogyny and racism in the pits, the sports have been pushing for a more inclusive environment where all people, regardless of race, gender, and age, are given an opportunity to take part in both the front lines and behind the scenes of the sports.

In recent years, there has been a very visible lack of diversity in the motorsports industry. This has been positively and is slowly being turned around. Mercedes' driver and seven-time world champion, Sir Lewis Hamilton, has been making efforts to voice out and be at the forefront of this advocacy.

In the matter of gender inclusiveness, Susie Wolff, the former professional racing driver, has helped establish a laddered opportunity pathway for women by leading a female-only racing series called the Formula 1 Academy. This allows women to access newer opportunities to participate in Formula 1.

Although no explicit rules prohibit the participation of female drivers in Formula 1, there has been a very limited presence and opportunity given to women for them to be seen in the F1 grid. As a matter of fact, it has been 32 years since a woman last competed in Formula One when Giovanna Amati raced in 1992. Cheers to a glorious seasonAs Formula 1 continues to evolve as a sport, it would not be surprising if the next seasons become more exciting and palpably appetizing to watch. Its influence in both automotive engineering and design, as well as its genius efforts to be more accessible, will surely make a great impact on how the next generation views the sport--- a competitive avenue where thrill and fairness transpire.
