N-word: Non-negotiableBy Kisly Moira PeraFor black people, "n*gga" is used as a term of endearment to recognize one as a friend or a "buddy". It is an adaption of the word "nigger", a Latin term for black people, inevitably the Africans, which was attached to the African slave trade in 1619, where Africans were sold as slaves mainly for the Americans, stripping their human rights off by treating them as no less than animals. The attachment of the word to this blood-soaked history seems to be under the shade of racial slur being treated as a surface level profanity. Evident to people, particularly the young, using the word often without any knowledge of the context behind this word.Our Non-existent Monopoly on WordsIt's simple; if you're not black, you shouldn't have a say about when or how to use the n-word. It's racist. Putting the focus on the etymology on the n-word, the black community had indeed, revived the slur and used the adapted word as a form of endearment. However, it does not give us, non-black people, the permission to use the term AT ALL. The reclaim does not change the history the word holds for their community. Moreover, the frequent use of the word in general as a provocative term, does not desensitize its naturally offensive connotation, hence the continuous backlash for its use. It is our responsibility to educate ourselves on these matters, especially when issues like these have been anchoring our progress in fostering a safe and inclusive society since time immemorial.The Weight of the WordI once asked a high school student (intentionally as he takes part of the highest exposure of false media in the spectrum) if he thinks people who say the n-word must face a consequence. His answer was "No, tapos na ang slavery ng black people matagal na so bakit kailangan mag suffer nang mga taong nag sasabi ng n-word ngayon? Lagi namang naririnig sa mga kanta." If you're struck, that makes two of us. Let's look at the facts.

In an exclusive cover of CNN Africa in 2017, they discovered slave auctions of Africans in Libya. Further information from news articles in 2017 states that 71% of migrants experience slavery, particular races mentioned were Eritreans, Somalis and Nigerians. Therefore no, black slavery is not completely eliminated in the modern world.

Slavery is not over, racism is not over. But say they are, the weight this word carry will never be lessened by any ounce. Black people cannot get over of the blood-soaked past because the world never allowed them to, and this is manifested in the modern day slavery, the use of n-word by the non-black people, and the general amount of disproportionate oblivion of the people of the mere facts such as the marginalization, and stagnant position of black people in the socio-economic ladder.
You Hang UpThe world of trends is so fast paced, "hypes" change from one minute to another. And while it is part of our human nature to seek dominance, accentuated in this generation as being seen "cool", you as a tech-savvy, and well rounded digital native must hang up to the call of this sense of "coolness" in using the n-word or literally any word that holds power in situating people or a community in harm or disgrace. Acknowledge that a "trend" in its own avenue can make you believe that because people does it, it's not wrong. It's high time we invest our attention to research on social issues as we are at the peak of information availability, there is no excuse to justify one's unawareness. The recurring issue of the use of the n-word is one of the most talked about topics through the years; history, articles, and other media resources are available. What else do we need?
