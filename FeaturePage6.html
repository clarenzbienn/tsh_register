The Happiest Centennial on EarthBy Gel Mendoza
Photo from https://www.architecturaldigest.com/story/disneyland-paris-sleeping-beauty-castle
Welcome aboard the steamboat, dear reader! Though you might not hear “Turkey in the Straw” playing while you read this, why don’t you allow yourselves to imagine—to reflect and realize that it’s a small world after all? Even if it did take a hundred years to build it.The world was vast, however, through the mental lens of Walt Disney, who decided to put his all into building his dream career of being an animator in the 1920s. In 1923, he and his brother Roy had came up with “Alice Comedies”, a series of shorts based off of Alice’s Adventures in Wonderland (which would then become its own full-length film made by the latter in 1951). He had soon found himself a distributor for the series in October of 1923, which then became the formal start of the Disney Brothers Cartoon Studio.

After four years, he had created his new cartoon hero, Oswald the Lucky Rabbit, a silent cartoon series about the everyday misadventures of the titular character, mostly including his love interest, Ortensia the Cat. But only after just a year and 26 episodes later, his distributor had decided to take the rights to Oswald for himself, along with most of Walt’s team of animators.

Disheartened, he then had to come up with a new character that he could truly call his own—and at his last of three attempts, he had created his new star—and one that would a sensation to all throughout generations. With the debut of “Steamboat Willie”, the very first Walt Disney short to ever include sound, also came the debut of what would become the studio’s main mascot to this day, Mickey Mouse.

“Steamboat Willie” would follow Mickey as a sidewheeler on a steamboat, who jams along with Minnie in the most comic way to the sheet music she brought along of “Turkey in the Straw”, and sending the message of finding the positive side of every situation despite unwanted setbacks and company.

The successes of Walt Disney would continue, bringing in many firsts for the company as well. In 1932, “Snow White and the Seven Dwarves” would make its big screen debut as the first animated movie to be shown in English and in full color. Also, Walt Disney would be the first to introduce the “mini-series concept” for television in 1954, with “Davy Crockett”, and the first to be able to develop a theme park intended for the entire family—Disneyland—in Anaheim, California, on July 17, 1955. To this day, it is still one of the most popular family destinations in the world—often dubbed “The Happiest Place on Earth.”

Even after Walt Disney’s death from lung cancer on December 15, 1966, Walt Disney Studios had, and still continued to make timeless classics both for television and the silver screen, with the latest addition to the distinguished roster being “Wish”, a 2023 film of wondrous magic and willpower that debuts a new Disney Princess, African-American Princess Asha (She is also the second African-American princess made by the company, after Tiana from 2009’s “The Princess and the Frog”).

On October of 2023, Walt Disney Studios celebrated its 100th Year Anniversary, with the release of the short film “Once Upon a Studio”. It showcases all of our favorite Disney characters ever created in the very same studio, all gathered together for to take one grand family picture. (Well, blood-curdling antagonists weren’t invited, though.)

Looking back, Walt Disney Studios has shaped generations of classics and continuous, failproof waves of nostalgia. One can truly spot a Disney movie or song from even miles away—every animated symbol that always finds time to reawaken our inner children that never really died, but only put to slumber.

With the Disney magic within each one of us, we can find ourselves making everything possible—whether it be a trip to Neverland, a tour around the magical Madrigal family home, finding people worth melting for, or just simply getting lost in Fantasia in the midst of troubled times.
And at the end of the day, whether or not our wishes upon a star come true, we can always seek comfort in faith, family, and fun—until we find ourselves with the happiest hearts on earth, filled to the brim with the kind of happiness that transcends many more centuries to come.
